A standing wave is a special interference pattern formed when two identical waves travel towards each other and interfere. A common occurrence of this is when a wave encounters a boundary and is reflected back (demonstrated in this simulation).

Although one source generated this wave, we now have two traveling waves: one outgoing and one reflected. You'll notice when these waves superimpose each other, two areas of particular interest are generated:

  • Areas where destructive interference is at a maximum (called nodes). The two waves are completely out of phase at this location, and the resultant amplitude from adding the waves together here is always zero.
  • Areas where constructive interference is at a maximum (called antinodes). The waves are completely in phase here, so that resultant amplitude becomes twice the original amplitude.

Again, the generation of nodes & antinodes is a result of the interfering waves being identical. The resultant wave appears stationary, hence, "standing".


A harmonic is one of several possible standing wave patterns for a given system (where the system can be a string, windpipe, etc.). The shape of the pattern and the number of nodes depends on the length of the system \(L\) and the type of boundaries at the ends:

$$\begin{align} \text{Both fixed / both free ends:}\qquad &L = n \frac{\lambda}{2},\quad n = 1,2,3\dots\\\\ \text{One fixed & one free end:}\qquad &L = n \frac{\lambda}{4}, \quad n = 1,3,5\dots \end{align} $$

Where \(\lambda\) is the wavelength, and \(n\) denotes all the possible harmonics (1st harmonic, 2nd harmonic, etc.)


Simulation Example

  • Set the Frequency to 8Hz and Pulse Width to 1λ.
  • Set both Right & Left side reflections to Fixed End.
  • Make sure that Properties sliders are set to No damping and Full reflection
  • Decrease to length of the medium to 1λ by dragging the boundary slider.

Now try sending a single pulse. Notice how the resultant wave is reflected and interferes with itself. Since \(L = \lambda\), that means \(n = 2\) and we are generating the 2nd harmonic.

In order to see the pattern properly, send a new pulse with a Pulse Width of 2λ. The 2nd harmonic standing wave should be a lot clearer now.

Now try sending a continuous wave (click SEND WAVE instead of SEND PULSE). Notice how the resultant wave gains more and more energy out of each consecutive reflection. This is because we are simulating with ideal conditions which aren't present in real life. In practice, waves will lose energy as they travel across space (damping) and will lose energy after each reflection (absorption by the boundary).
